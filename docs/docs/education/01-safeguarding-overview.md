## From Reactive to Proactive Safeguarding

Reactive security responds to incidents after they have occurred. A DSL reviewing CCTV footage of a safeguarding incident is gathering evidence of harm already done. Proactive safeguarding means the system continuously flags anomalies before they become incidents.

KCSiE (Keeping Children Safe in Education) requires schools to have "appropriate safeguarding arrangements proportionate to risk." Technology is part of that provision. A school whose CCTV system is offline, whose visitor records are paper-based, and whose access doors cannot be remotely locked has a demonstrable gap between statutory obligation and actual provision.

## The Fragmented System Problem

The typical UK school estate has:
- A legacy CCTV system installed under a capital budget, running on an isolated network
- A door entry system on a separate protocol not connected to CCTV management
- A school Wi-Fi network designed for learning, used as an afterthought for security systems

When these systems fail to communicate:
- A lockdown requires staff to operate three disconnected systems simultaneously under stress
- A camera goes offline because shared Wi-Fi fails; the outage is undiscovered for days
- Access doors cannot be locked remotely because they have no central management connection
- Visitor logs in paper form cannot be searched for a Subject Access Request

## The 2026 Pressure Landscape

**MAT consolidation:** Multi-academy trusts managing security across multiple sites require consistent, centrally manageable systems. Non-standardised legacy provision creates compliance risk at scale.

**Budget constraints:** Capital investment must be justified on compliance value and operational efficiency, not hardware alone.

**Martyn's Law:** The Terrorism (Protection of Premises) Act 2025 creates statutory emergency preparedness obligations for schools. Enforcement begins April 2027. Most schools are in scope.

## Key Statistic

Approximately 87% of school security incidents occur outside standard teaching hours — evening, weekend, and holiday periods when staff presence is minimal and fragmented systems are least monitored.
